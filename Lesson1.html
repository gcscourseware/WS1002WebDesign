The first decision is the platform that will host and manage your content. There are two main categories:

Overview: Wix is a popular, all-in-one platform known for its extreme ease of use and visual editor. It includes hosting, domain registration, and customer support.

  • Pros: Excellent for beginners, fast setup, extensive template library, no coding required.
  • Cons: Less flexibility for large, custom projects; difficult to migrate content off the platform later.
  • Best For: Small businesses, portfolios, service providers, and anyone prioritizing speed and simplicity.

Overview: WordPress is the world's most popular Content Management System (CMS), offering unparalleled control and scalability.

  • Pros: Unlimited customization via themes and plugins, excellent scalability, strong community support, full data ownership.
  • Cons: Requires more technical setup (hosting, security, maintenance); higher learning curve.
  • Best For: Large blogs, complex custom applications, high-traffic e-commerce (using WooCommerce).

üéØ 2. Defining Goals and Target Audience

Before designing, you must answer two fundamental questions: "What is this site for?" and "Who is going to use it?"

Your goal determines every design choice, from the homepage image to the call-to-action (CTA) buttons:

  • Informational: Goal is to educate (e.g., news site, blog). Key metric: Time on page, bounce rate.
  • Transactional: Goal is to sell products or services (e.g., e-commerce, software subscription). Key metric: Conversion rate.
  • Lead Generation: Goal is to capture customer information (e.g., contact forms, email signups). Key metric: Form submission rate.
  • Branding/Portfolio: Goal is to showcase work and build trust (e.g., photographer, architect). Key metric: Gallery views, download portfolio clicks.

üó∫Ô∏è 3. Planning Website Structure and Content

A well-planned site uses structure to guide the user seamlessly toward your primary goal.

The sitemap is a hierarchical map of all pages on your site. It defines the main navigation structure. A simple sitemap should look like this:

- Home
  |-- About Us
  |-- Services (Parent Page)
      |-- Service A Detail Page
      |-- Service B Detail Page
  |-- Portfolio / Gallery
  |-- Blog / News
  |-- Contact Us

Key Principle: Users should be able to reach any page on your site in three clicks or less.

Wireframing is sketching the layout of a page *without* colors, images, or final text. It focuses purely on the placement of elements (navigation, headings, image placeholders, CTA buttons).

  • Purpose: To ensure that the most important elements (based on your goal) are prominently placed.
  • Tip: Always wireframe the mobile view first. Mobile users are the priority for modern web design.

Content is king! For each page identified in your sitemap, define the following:

  1. SEO Title and Meta Description: How the page appears in search results.
  2. Primary Headline (H1): Clear and benefit-driven.
  3. Call-to-Action (CTA): What button should appear (e.g., "Shop Now," "Learn More," "Get a Quote").
  4. Media Requirements: Which specific photos, videos, or graphics are needed.
